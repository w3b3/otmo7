DOC 001 // Engineering Operating Model

How we
work.

A short guide to how engineering operates on this team.
8 principles  ·  high autonomy  ·  high signal  ·  high leverage.

01 // Communication / Rhythm

We don't run dailies.

Short, unstructured, transparent updates throughout the day — the remote equivalent of bumping into a colleague in the hallway. Asynchronous when possible, synchronous when it actually helps.

Driven by what you need to make progress, not by a calendar invite. The scrum daily is theater when the team communicates continuously.

02 // Feedback / Cadence

We don't wait for retros to give feedback.

Asking peers for feedback every week — small or large, process or behavior, communication or pace. Retros are a backstop, not the cadence.

Asking for feedback is not weakness
It's a signal that you take the peer relationship seriously — and that you believe your colleagues' observations are worth your time. The team that receives feedback weekly adapts weekly. The team that waits for retros adapts quarterly.
No minimum size
A two-sentence observation about how someone communicated in a meeting is worth surfacing. Withholding small feedback because it doesn't feel significant enough is how small things compound into large ones.
03 // Tooling / AI

We don't treat AI tools as optional.

Using them daily and intensely. This is non-negotiable. AI tools don't replace engineering judgment — they raise the price of not having any.

01
Build skills — repeatable, codified workflows for the actions you do constantly
Ticket creation, commits, PRs, smoke checks, incident reports, RCAs. When an agent can produce working code in any language or stack, the differentiator is whether you can compound that into repeatable leverage.
02
Apply critical thinking — don't merge the first thing that compiles
The output is a starting point, not the answer. Technically correct and solving the right problem are not the same question. Your job is to know the difference.
03
Steer — toward architecture that won't collapse in two months
Agents optimize for the immediate prompt. You optimize for the system over time. Architectural taste is not something the agent holds. You hold it.
04
Recognize — when the solution is correct but solves the wrong problem
Languages and stacks now have low barriers. Architectural taste, product instinct, and the discipline to interrogate what an agent gives you — those are the high-value assets. That's where your time should go.
+ Curiosity

You actually use the product

You care and say something when it's awkward. You ask why a user would do the thing the ticket describes. You don't wait for QA, PM, or anyone to tell you what's broken. You notice.

+ Ownership

A module has someone

Someone whose name comes up when it's discussed — who knows its history, its sharp edges, and where it's going. Not because they were assigned it. Because they took it.

04 // Pragmatism / Tooling Discipline

We refine because we must — not because we want to.

Under-utilizing something is not ideal. We build the roads we need to use, if we can maintain them. We buy or pay someone to do it if it makes more sense.

The business will drive most decisions. Your voice will steer a few. Be vocal.

05 // Ownership / Direction-Setting

We don't have PMs.

We don't outsource direction-setting to a product manager who tells engineers what to build. We have engineers who own the product.

20%
Leadership
Touch points. Strategic hints and direction.
80%
Engineers
Driving value through real ownership of their product or module. The deciding voice on what to build next is, most of the time, yours.
Architecture review — every week, not optional
Evolution of the system is shared and discussed openly. If a system is changing and the team doesn't know how, that's the failure mode we're avoiding. Not a formal ceremony — a continuous, documented conversation.
Ideas come from engineers — not handed down from above
Product teams here are composed of engineers delivering value. This is not a slogan — it's a structural commitment backed by how reviews and roadmaps actually work.
06 // Observability / Signals

We wire for signals.

Every feature, every module, every service emits something readable from day one. Logs that say what happened. Metrics that say how often and how fast. Traces that say where time was spent. Errors that surface with enough context to act on.

We don't treat dashboards, logs, traces, and metrics as something to add later when there's time. The absence of a signal is a signal.

Wire it before you ship it
Ownership without signals is just opinion. Curiosity without telemetry is just guessing. Instrumentation is what gives your judgment something real to work with. This is what makes engineer-driven product decisions honest.

Decouple deploy from release

Code goes to production behind feature flags. Exposure is a separate, controlled decision. Deploy and release are two different events — treat them that way.

Ship dark, then light up gradually

Internal first. Slice of users next. Watch the signals. Roll forward or roll back based on what you see — not based on what you hope.

Run experiments where they matter

When two reasonable approaches exist and the answer isn't obvious, set up the comparison. Let real usage decide. In architecture review, the first question is "what did the telemetry show?" — not "what do you think?"

Decisions are data-backed by default

Opinion is cheap. Data is not. When you propose a change, the question is not whether it seems right — it's whether the signals confirm it.

07 // Review / Evidence

We review with Claude Code.

Agents catch what tired human eyes miss, and they do it consistently. The goal is evidence-backed review — not ceremony.

PR descriptions are full and evidence-backed

What changed, why, what was tested, what was verified, screenshots or logs where they help. The description is the artifact future-you will read when something breaks.

All systems are accessible via CLI or MCP

If your environment can only be inspected through a GUI, that's a gap to close. Hard debugging cases will always need a human — but the path to that human should be terminal-first.

If a human reviews it, they ran it

No reproduction steps — the PR is not ready. "Looks good" is not a review. Ran it, watched it, verified the signals moved — that's a review.

Reviews happen with Claude Code

Agents catch what tired human eyes miss, and they do it consistently. Use them. The review is the last gate before production — don't treat it as a formality.

08 // Output / Artifacts

Transparency and flexibility.

Every effort produces an artifact — and closes the loop. Daily focused progress and feedback, where the loop closes with evidence, not with a status update that says something was done.

Surface your contribution — intentionally
It is not by accident or chance that we are all here. If a day of effort produced no artifact, something went wrong — either the work wasn't worth it, or the output wasn't captured. If a PR merged but no signal moved, the work isn't done. Both are problems worth surfacing.
We all win if we all row the same cadence
Toward the same end goal. The loop closes with evidence — not with a Friday dump of what was done.

Several points, one underlying claim.

High autonomy — the deciding voice on what to build is usually yours.
High signal — wire before you ship; the absence of signal is a signal.
High leverage — build skills, steer agents, close loops with evidence.
